1.1 Overview of datasets

This projects data consists of two datasets recorded from a Minecraft research server (Very fun!).

players.csv

The first dataset is called Players.CSV, which contains one row per unique player (Totalling 196 players) and provides the demographic and some gameplay information. It includes:

  1. experience self-reported gaming skill level (Amateur, Regular, Veteran, or Pro).
  2. subscribe whether the player subscribed to the newsletter (TRUE/FALSE).
  3. hashedEmail a unique identifier used to link players to their sessions.
  4. played_hours the total number of hours the player spent on the server.
  5. name the player's chosen in-game name.
  6. gender players gender (Male/Female).
  7. Age players age in years (ranging from 9 to ~47).

Additional note: Upon review, I noticed that the data is self-reported/automatically recorded, so some inconsistencies may potentially appear (For example, very low played_hours for Pro players).

sessions.csv

The second file contains one row per gameplay session (Totalling 1535 sessions). Each record captures:

  1. hashedEmail links the session to a player.
  2. start_time human-readable timestamp for when the session began.
  3. end_time human-readable timestamp for when the session ended.
  4. original_start_time Unix timestamp version of start_time.
  5. original_end_time Unix timestamp version of end_time.

Relationship between files

Both of these files can be linked using hashedEmail col, allowing us to combine a players demographics with their gameplay behaviour and habbits, which is important when creating the main response variable for later modeling (For example, total play hours or total sessions per player).

(2) Questions

Broad Question

The big question I'm looking at for this project is figuring out which players give us the best data. Since the Minecraft server is part of an ongoing study, some people naturally create way more gameplay information than others. If we can pin down what those high-data players look like, the research team can be smarter about who we try to recruit next.

Specific Question

My specific predictive question is whether we can use a player's basic characteristics to forecast if they'll turn into a high-usage player. For this project, I'm defining a "high-usage player" very clearly: it's anyone who finishes in the top 25% of all players based on their total hours played on the server.

My formal question is:

Can a players experience level, age, and gender predict whether they will become a high-usage player (top 25% of total hours played)?

(4) Methods and Plan

I will be using the K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN) to predict whether a player becomes a high-usage player (top 25% of total hours played) based on their experience level, age, and gender. Since my response variable (high_usage) is binary, KNN works well as a classification method.

K-nearest neighbor is a simple and relatively intuitive model that classifies a player based on the characteristics of the nearest players to them. The idea is that players who are similar in terms of age, gender, and experience level may also behave similarly in terms of how much they play. KNN does not assume a straight-line or linear relationship between predictors and the outcome, which is helpful here because the differences between player types are not necessarily linear.

Certain Assumptions and considerations

KNN has a few important assumptions that I will need to keep in mind:

  1. Predictors should be on similar scales. Age is numeric, while gender and experience are categorical, so I will need to properly encode these and scale all numeric variables so that distance calculations are fair.

  2. KNN can be sensitive to imbalanced classes. In my dataset, the high-usage players only make up 25% of the data by definition, so I need to keep in mind that the model might lean toward predicting the majority group.

  3. KNN depends on the choice of K. Choosing the right number of neighbours is important, and the model can behave very differently with a small vs. large K.

How I will process the data

Before applying KNN, I will first need to convert experience and gender into factor variables. Then scale the numeric variables (Age) so they are on comparable ranges as well as handle the two missing Age values from earlier. Lastly, I will create the **high_usage variable as done in Section 3.

Train/test and split model selection

To evaluate this model, I will need to split the data into a 70/30 trainingtest split, train the KNN model using only the training set, then choose the best value of K using cross-validation on the training data, and finally evaluate performance on the test set using accuracy (or another classification metric).
